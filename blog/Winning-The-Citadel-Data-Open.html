Winning the Citadel Data Open


December 13th, 2022

Introduction

Last week three friends and I competed in the 2022 Citadel Data Open Championship, a premier Machine Learning and Data Science competition with a $100,000 cash grand prize. Yesterday, our team was humbled to be named the 1st place global champions. Today, I decided to share a brief summary of our experience.

After winning Citadel‚Äôs West Coast Fall Datathon over a year ago with an NLP and transfer-learning powered SVM clickbait classifer, my friends and I earned ourselves a ticket to the elusive Data Open Championship. Twenty-one other top teams from across ten regional Datathons were invited to compete among us for the $100,000 grand prize. The competition has been described as "the largest and most prestigious university-level data science competition in the world" with previous top teams including PhD students from MIT, Stanford, Berkeley, Yale, and Cambridge.


After much anticipation, on November 30th the competition‚Äôs problem statement and dataset were finally released: to analyze America‚Äôs postsecondary educational institutions using the IPEDS dataset, which includes a depth of granular information about school demographics, finances, admissions, and completions. We were then given four days to pose a novel research question, answer it using mathematic, statistical, and computational modeling, then write a technical report detailing our methods and findings.

Scope

Given the prohibitively short time-span of the competition, our team quickly decided to focus on an impactful yet straightforward question that we could feasibly analyze with meaningful depth, precision, and rigor. After an initial survey of the IPEDS data and potential auxiliary data sources, we decided to focus our efforts on exploring how various institution-level factors correlate with graduating students with high incomes and low debt. We settled on this question both due to its appeal as a particularly relevant topic and because we believed we had enough high-quality data to support a robust analysis of the subject after pulling in supplementary datasets from The College Scorecard and PayScale.

Models

We used a Robust Weighted Least Squares Two-Way Fixed Effects Model to analyze trends in our longitudinal population-scale data while controlling for and quantifying heterogeneity of temporal trends and institutional differences. We combined this analysis with the popular techniques of k-means clustering and multiple linear regression to better understand institutional commonalities and cross-sectional relationships. To ensure robustness, we took care to vet our models against the Gauss-Markov assumptions for statistical validity, including Multicollinearity, Specification Error, Heteroskedasticity, and Residual Normality, as well as corrections for Outliers and Overfitting.

Interpretation

To make sure we could draw meaningful results from our analysis, we selected our models with an emphasis on interpretability. We were able to reverse-engineer human-interpretable labels for our k-means clusters by examining the feature distributions over each cluster and extract the significant standardized feature coefficients and fixed effects of our regressions to quantify relationships between our variables. Since we had separate models for early-career pay, mid-career pay, and debt, we were also able to analyze patterns in how institutional factors affected different dependent variables, which gave rise to some memorable findings. After several back-to-back all-day grind sessions, we were able to round off our research and technical report in time to submit a whole 4 minutes before the deadline.


Conclusion

The Data Open Championship was an incredible opportunity to put our love of mathematical analysis to work exploring important questions about our world. We had an amazing time with this series of competitions and are excited for what future adventures lie ahead! I hope you enjoyed hearing a bit about our process.
